Sources should be cited either in the parenthetical in-text system developed by the Modern Language Association of America ("MLA style") or the notes-and-bibliography system developed by *The Chicago Manual of Style* ("Chicago notes-and-bibliography").
MLA style is the default system for university coursework in literary studies in the United States.
Chicago's notes-and-bibliography system is widely used for published scholarship in humanities disciplines, including literary studies.

For MLA style see the MLA Style Center's [Quick Guide to Works Cited](https://style.mla.org/works-cited/works-cited-a-quick-guide/) or
the Purdue Online Writing Lab's guide to [MLA In-Text Citations](https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/research_and_citation/mla_style/mla_formatting_and_style_guide/mla_in_text_citations_the_basics.html).
For the Chicago notes-and-bibliography system see *The Chicago Manual of Style*'s [Citation Quick Start Guide](https://www.chicagomanualofstyle.org/tools_citationguide/citation-guide-1.html).

For guidance on citing the output of generative artificial intelligence applications, see MLA's ["How do I cite generative AI in MLA style?"](https://style.mla.org/citing-generative-ai-updated-revised/)
and *The Chicago Manual of Style*'s Q&A on [citing content developed or generated by artificial intelligence](https://www.chicagomanualofstyle.org/qanda/data/faq/topics/Documentation/faq0422.html).

Students are encouraged to use reference management software.
I use [Zotero](https://www.zotero.org/).
It is free and open-source.

This syllabus uses an uncommon parenthetical variant of the Chicago system [see @ChicagoManualStyle2024, 12.77], but the format of the [Bibliography](#bibliography) is standard and may be taken as a model.
